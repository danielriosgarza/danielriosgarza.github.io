# What is and why do we need microbial systems ecology?

## A microbial planet

The nearly 4 billion years of our planet's living history is essentially the history of the systems that we generically call microbiomes: assemblages of interconnected microbes continuously interacting with the Earth's living and non-living realms. Major planetary changes were driven, buffered, and always tolerated by the microbiomes that are likely thrive long after our species has vanished.

Microbiomes are the planet's biogeochemical engineers[^fn-1]. From the depths of the ocean to the bodies of animals and plants, they orchestrate how energy and matter flows across all trophic levels and chemical forms. While some rogue microbes, often part-timers, cause diseases and inflict immensurable economic losses to human society.

Any common sense list of the current threats faced by humanity - climate change, disease outbreaks, environmental degradation, biodiversity loss, poverty, and food insecurity - are closely linked to the functioning of the Earth's microbiomes. Unsurprisingly, the innovative and promising solutions to these threats involve managing microbiomes in a way or another.

In fact, some of humanities' most critical challenges can only be solved if we learn how to become efficient microbiome keepers (or stewards[^fn-2]).

## Are we the Earth's microbiome keepers?

inadvertently, we have long become the Earth's microbiome keepers. But, like Cain to Abel, not of the good sorts. Our current era, the Anthropocene, is an era where human activity significantly changed the planet's flow of energy and matter to the point of no return. While human society is causing these changes, they are far from our rational control. A simple reason is that between planetary fluxes and us, there are microbiomes. When we raise livestock for food, their gut microbiomes produce the greenhouse gas methane; When we produce more agricultural crops, the nitrogen and organic matter accumulate in the water causing algal blooms;  When treat wastewater, ammonia oxidizing bacteria emit the greenhouse gas Nitrous oxide, whose molecules remain in the atmosphere for over a hundred years, When we burn fossil fuels for energy, we acidify the ocean altering microbial activity and modifying the cycles of Nitrogen, oxygen, and carbon[^fn-3]; When we treat disease with antibiotics, we promote microbial resistance and the emergence of untreatable superbugs.

There are those of us--perhaps the few--that believe we could mitigate and even reverse some of these changes by accounting for microbiomes in important global decisions[^fn-3]. In my opinion, with large-scale investments and scientific breakthroughs we could go even further and develop strategies the direct the flows of matter and energy, at planetary scale, towards a sustainable future for our species (as opposed to extinction, for instance). However, controlling the Earth's biogeochemical engineers towards a more sustainable future is, today, as far-fetched of a goal as colonizing another planet or time travelling. Even if microbes are at the reach of our atomic microscopes and next generation sequencers, rationally controlling their ecosystems still belongs to the realm of science fiction, or, at best, to the realm of untested theoretical models.

## Microbiomes and science

Make no mistake. We have a wealth of knowledge about microbes, having studied them for centuries. Nobel prizes have been won, honorific scientific societies created, science has covered broad aspects of their existence, from molecules to ecosystems. Key discoveries are made almost on a daily basis. For example, we know the detailed molecular structure of microbial proteins that drive the Earth's biogeochemical cycles, we have sequenced microbial genomes and metagenomes in the order of millions, we have detailed knowledge of model microorganisms, hundreds to thousands of metabolic models, culture and enrichment protocols, hundreds of thousands of isolates, metatranscriptomes, metaproteomes, and metabolomes. Even well-resolved time series are becoming increasingly common. However, the "Terra incognita" lies in understanding how this knowledge fits together. How much and what parts do we need to understand before we can rationally influence the entire system?

First, inspired by Yang-Yu Liu's beautiful compendium, I believe we need minimal wiring diagrams for some of the Earth's complex microbiomes. By "minimal," I mean wiring diagrams that provide us sufficient control over well-defined effects, significantly outweighing undesired side-effects. It might be long before we can obtain minimal wiring diagrams to divert planetary fluxes towards a sustainable future or for reverting microbiome-related diseases like colitis and bacterial vaginosis. We can, however, start by pragmatically pursuing the ability to design, control, and execute well-defined microbiome control operations in model systems like gut-on-a-chip or intelligent, responsive bioreactors. I mean learning operational control with theory and experiments of complex microbiomes that we can program like a computer that executes virtually any program within its memory and processing capabilities. In my opinion, obtaining model microbiome systems with such minimal wiring diagrams is a fundamental step for our understanding of biology, regardless of its utility or lack thereof. Quoting J. Oppenheimer:

"It is a profound and necessary truth that the deep things in science are not found because they are useful; they are found because it was possible to find them."

Then why not send out the great sailing expeditions to chart the Terra incognita? In the past few years, several research groups have done precisely that. The first expeditions reveal some of the beauty and complexity of microbiome organization and functioning, albeit not yet elucidating minimal wiring diagrams per se.

## Charting the Terra incognita

We usually want to understand microbiomes over time, in the long run. For example, the species composition and metabolites in the gut are dynamic variables, whose quantities depend on the game played by their sources and sinks. Given a time scale, they settle into a dynamic behavior: increasing if production or inflow exceeds their consumption, outflow, or death--in the case of microbes; decreasing if they fall short; they may also oscillate, remain constant, or even be chaotic. The regime depends on our time scale and on how we renew the system.

Serial passages are frequently chosen to study microbiome regimes in vitro. Here, where a fraction of the community is propagated into fresh medium for several passages--generally until the dynamic and endpoint quantities of a passage are virtually undistinguishable from the previous one.
